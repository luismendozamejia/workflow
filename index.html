1. Check the contract type

First, verify whether the customer has a Buy Now contract or a Leasing contract.

1.1 If it is a Buy Now contract

  • Check if the system is still inside the warranty period.
  • If it is under warranty: provide full support to the customer and handle the issue as a standard warranty case.
  • If it is no longer under warranty:
    • Do your best to support and guide the customer anyway.
    • If an on-site visit or additional work is required, inform the customer that the intervention will be done under quotation.
    • Use the dedicated ‚Äúout of warranty / quotation needed‚Äù template to explain the situation.

1.2 If it is a Leasing contract

  • Check if the customer is up to date with the payments.
  • Check if the system is still within the warranty period.

If payments are up to date and the system is in warranty, provide support to the customer.

If payments are not up to date, verify if the payment delay is caused by the technical issue.

  • If the delay is clearly caused by the incident: provide support to the customer.
  • If the delay is not related to the incident:
    • Explain to the customer that it is necessary to catch up with the payments in order to continue providing support.
    • Use the dedicated ‚ÄúLeasing / overdue payments‚Äù template to communicate this.
Tip: Always document in the ticket whether the payment status and warranty period were checked, and which template was used.

3. The plant does not produce enough

When the customer reports that the plant is not producing enough, start by checking the installed power and the inverter nominal power.

  • The maximum production is mainly limited by:
    • The inverter nominal power.
    • The total DC power of the PV modules.

Example for customer explanation (use in a template):

Imagine a Huawei SUN2000-3.68 inverter. The inverter has a nominal AC power of 3.68 kW.

The customer has installed 10 panels of 500 W each, which gives a total DC power of approximately 5 kW.

Even though the total panel power is 5 kW, the plant will never exceed 3.68 kW of instantaneous production, because this is the limit of the inverter.

This does not mean the system is wrong; it means the design is made so that the inverter can reach its nominal power more often and for more hours during the day, improving the overall energy yield throughout the year.

Suggestion: include a simple chart or screenshot from the monitoring portal when explaining power limitations, so the customer can visually understand why the curve is ‚Äúcapped‚Äù at the inverter‚Äôs nominal power.
  • Check alerts, events or alarms on the monitoring system by:
    • The inverter nominal power.
    • The total DC power of the PV modules.
